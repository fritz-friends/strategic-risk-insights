There’s a paradox in any recession: in the beginning, overall wages go up, not down. The fall comes later. Why? The answer lies in how people lose jobs during a recession. Those at the lowest end of the wage scale lose their jobs first, which has the effect of temporarily raising wages elsewhere until layoffs hit those in the middle and upper distributions of the wage scale. The same held true in the pandemic, low wage workers lost their jobs first.

**What did I think would happen**? For a number of reasons, from jobs that could not be done remotely to the lack of health care alternatives, I thought that low wage workers would play an important role in recovery: the sooner we could re-hire these workers, to sooner we would recover. However, I also noted “the devastation is worse, and recovery will take longer in low wage communities,” when discussing demographic considerations to recovery. Any economic recovery would slowly trickle down to this income group.

**What happened?** This prediction is tricky to reflect on. Part of it appears to be true, devastation from the pandemic has been worse for those on the lowest rungs of the economic ladder, but part of it is false, recovery shouldn’t take longer for low-wage workers. With the aid of government programs – PPP, stimulus payments, unemployment benefits, and moratoriums on evictions and foreclosures – those in lower economic industries have been able to survive the pandemic. However, critics have noted that healthy unemployment benefits through the pandemic have replaced—and sometimes exceeded—the regular wages of this group.

The part that’s false is that the recovery would take longer in low-wage jobs. Today there are plentiful opportunities for workers to return, from restaurant services to unskilled construction laborers. I read last week about a manufacturer in Maine who turned down $1 million in new business because he can’t find enough workers to make the goods. The recovery, which appears to be gathering steam at all income levels and across all industries, may be slowed by the dearth of people willing to work low-wage, entry level jobs.

**So What?** What’s the **bottom line**? The labor supply has shrunk. Either last year’s low wage workers have moved on to other opportunities, or they’re sitting on the sidelines because of generous unemployment benefits. For those seeking to hire at this level, expect to pay more in wages and expect a longer wait time to get the people you need and want.
