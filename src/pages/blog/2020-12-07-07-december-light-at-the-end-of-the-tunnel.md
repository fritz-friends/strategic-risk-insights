I attended a presentation this past week with public health experts. They discussed the current status of COVID-19 and the state of the vaccine. The good news is that there’s clear light at the end of the tunnel. The bad news is that there’s still a lot of tunnel left to navigate. In this weeks’ post I’ll focus on the light at the end of the tunnel and share with you what I know about the vaccine and some of the challenges and uncertainties I see just ahead. The vaccine offers hope for an end to the pandemic, but I see five key challenges between now and the end.

**Different vaccines, different distribution**

The Pfizer vaccine requires super-cold storage for it to be effective. The experts talked about their efforts to procure dry ice and specialized freezers to hold the vaccine. Because of this, I’d expect to see the Pfizer vaccine used heavily in urban and suburban areas. Distribution will be through hospitals, clinics, and pharmacies that either have or can acquire super-cold refrigeration.

The Moderna vaccine, preserved through regular refrigeration, should prove far more flexible and may become a vaccine that can be “taken to the people.” This vaccine would be great for schools, rural areas, and even inner cities where having a more flexible vaccine will be an advantage. The technology of the vaccine means that geography will matter.

**Bottom line:** While not a nightmare, distributing the vaccine while adhering to all protocols will require careful coordination and constant effort. It will be a delicate dance.

**Effective vaccination requires two doses**

It’s a two-shot vaccine, and those shots must be given about 4 weeks apart. Logistics and compliance will be critical factors in success. Two shots double the number of shots that need to be given (think 600 million instead of 300 million) and scheduling all those appointments will require the health care system to be at the top of its game.

Logistics will be a challenge, but nothing compared to compliance. Ask any pharmacist or physician about medication problems and they’ll tell you the biggest challenge is getting people to 1) take their medication as directed, and 2) finish the entire course of the medication. Even for those with chronic conditions, compliance is an issue. State and local governments, as well as business and community leaders will all have to work to educate people on the need for vaccine compliance.

**Bottom Line:** The biggest challenge to getting past the pandemic will be human nature, and compliance with all vaccine protocols. Wise leaders will spend time focusing as much on the “how” of vaccination as the “why.”

**Reticence and wariness about the vaccine is non-trivial**

The experts gave a range of 24-45% of survey respondents who indicated they won’t take the vaccine. No one gave reasons for these numbers, but two come to mind. These vaccines were developed a breakneck speed, and many are likely concerned about the safety and efficacy of the vaccine. The FDA approval process requires efficacy, and both the agency and the companies will focus on safety during the final review and approval process. If early recipients show no adverse reactions, this first group of hesitant vaxxers should see the value of the vaccine.

The second group are those making a political or social statement by refusing to receive the vaccine. This group will likely hold out for a long time and may only be swayed to vaccinate if there are real consequences for not getting the vaccine. Government leaders have no legal basis to mandate a vaccine, but the market may provide strong incentives to vaccinate. Rumor has it that airlines may require proof of vaccination to board, and that would provide a real incentive for some holdouts to vaccinate. Many of these folks will eventually receive the vaccine, after they get tired enough of living in a pandemic world.

**Bottom line:** Education about the benefits and safety of the vaccine will be essential at all levels of society. All of us in leadership roles need to talk openly, but optimistically, about our experiences with the vaccine. As skeptics see that it’s safe, most will overcome their reticence and get vaccinated.

**Timing is everything, and still pretty uncertain.**

The vaccine will roll out in phases, with health care workers first in line. This is as it should be, no doubt. Those who work in long-term care facilities, such as assisted living and nursing homes, and residents of those facilities will also be in the first vaccine group. Expect some delays here, mostly as thousands of facilities and professionals come down the learning curves of compliance and logistics. Essential workers come next, but as of today, that’s a pretty uncertain term.

One expert on the call noted that there are over 850 job titles listed by the Department of Labor, and figuring out the priority order will take time and cause some grief. After all, everyone considers their work “essential.” The bulk of the adult population will receive the vaccine sometime in the spring or early summer. I’ve heard ranges from April to July. That’s a big window. Many issues come into play here, from the challenges of manufacturing and storing hundreds of millions of doses to processing those at the front of the line.

**Bottom line:** Don’t ditch your mask just yet. Patience is a virtue, and plan on living with your current restrictions until sometime next summer.

In a post I wrote in early April, I discounted the notion that the economy would be open by Easter of 2020. I said, “maybe by the 4th of July, and then haltingly.” As we approach Christmas and still see lots of tunnel, I’ll stand by my prediction of the 4th of July. I just missed it by a year. I hope that by then most of us will be vaccinated, and the economy will begin to accelerate and Americans, and the world, can put the pandemic behind them and move into a new normal.
