With the Thanksgiving week behind us, and gratitude expressed to those who make our lives better, we’re ready to return to a focus on 2021 and beyond. I am grateful for the powerful American spirit and I’m convinced that if we build upon that spirit, we’ll see success in our endeavors, in our lives, our families, work, and our communities. For this week’s post, I want to focus on one element of that spirit: faith in the future.

Just over 80 years ago an early management guru, Chester Barnard, identified several key functions that executives play in leading their organizations forward. He knew something of executive work, having moved through the ranks and sat in the chair as President of New Jersey Bell. For Barnard, the best executives had the ability “to inspire cooperative personal decision by creating faith: faith in common understanding, faith in the probability of success, faith in the ultimate satisfaction of personal motives, faith in the integrity of objective authority, faith in the superiority of common purpose as a personal aim of those who partake in it.”

Faith is always future oriented and let me outline three ways you can have faith in your organization’s future, and how you can inspire faith in those who work for and with you.

**Focus on the future**

You can’t create faith in the future unless you look to the future. Last March, when the world turned upside down, everyone scrambled to adjust, and our focus became incredibly shortsighted. For many leaders, getting through the day was enough, and we dealt with unexpected challenges at every turn. Everyone was groping their way forward, and there was no way to have faith in the future if your team couldn’t survive the present. That was then, and that was fine. Today, however, you’ve got almost nine months of experience.

Coca-Cola CFO John Murphy expressed, what is for me, the wrong attitude about the road just ahead. He said, “The name of the game is to stay very focused on what we can control in the here and now, particularly in anticipation of potentially more lockdowns in the winter period.” NO! Your frontline people should be focused on the here and now, middle managers should be focused on making and hitting realistic budgets, and you—the leader of the organization—should be focused on the future, not the here and now. We use the following graphic to help executives see their role.

![](/img/30-nov-_-management-time-lines.jpg)

If your people and your teams learned how to deal with lockdowns last winter, they should have a playbook for what the Winter might bring. What they don’t have, and only you can provide, is the perspective to be looking beyond the winter, and even beyond next summer. Your organizational perch should give you a better view of the future. Take advantage of that perspective and look ahead.

**Bottom Line:** Don’t duplicate the work of those who work for you. Use your position to look down the road a ways.

**Trust in the power of creativity and ingenuity**

A common trait among many executives is a great work ethic, coupled with the notion that “if it is to be, it is up to me.” Self-reliance and a great work ethic have helped put you where you are; however, when you’re planning for the future, you need to change one word of the self-reliance mantra: “if it is to be, it is up to *us*.” Faith in the future means inspiring those who work for you to have a stake in that future, and to help you build it. Your team, and the people who work throughout your organization, have a wealth of creativity and they can find solutions to most tactical, and even some strategic, challenges you face.

A generation ago, Lee Iacocca took the reins at a bankrupt Chrysler corporation. He knew his team needed to solve the problems that had brought the firm to its knees, and he knew his team would have to innovate and take risks to solve those problems. In an early executive meeting, Iacocca is reported to have told his team that he knew creativity and innovation would create both hits and misses, but he hoped they would “make $5 million mistakes, not $50 million ones.” That spirit of making small bets on the future helped turn the company around.

In today’s environment, cash is king, and wise leaders steward every penny to create value. You won’t build faith in the future, and inspire your team to share that faith, if you fail to innovate and creatively solve problems. You won’t win if you team makes “$50 million” mistakes, so encourage them to make bets in the $5 million range.

**Bottom Line:** Share your faith in the future by allowing your people to contribute to building that future. Your team’s collective knowledge is an engine to leverage.

**Communicate realistic optimism**

Coronavirus continues to rage. Hurricanes and foul weather have devasted the South, and snow and cold will have their way in the Northeast, in the Plains, and in the Mountains. It’s easy to see optimism as a naïve, Pollyanna vision of a future that looks unlikely. Remember, your goal is to inspire faith in the future, and that requires a bedrock personal belief of optimism. The most helpful optimism is realistic optimism, one that admits the scale and scope of challenges, but one that stubbornly holds to the belief that better days are in store.

Begin your meetings and your communications with a frank assessment of where things stand. Optimism doesn’t mean pretending problems don’t exist! Optimists see the problems and challenges and they don’t shrink in the face of daunting odds. Discuss those odds, but then move on. Make plans, get agreement, and then move forward. End your meetings, and begin your work, with a commitment to doing all you can to solve problems and create a better future. That’s faith.

**Bottom Line:** Optimism is key, but carry with you a realistic outlook. Clear communication with your teams about what’s coming will help you clear the hurdles as they come at you.
