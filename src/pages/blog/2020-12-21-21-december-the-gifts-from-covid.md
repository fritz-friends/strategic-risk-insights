It’s Christmas this week, and we’re all looking forward to seeing what Santa will leave under the tree and in our stockings. Since it’s the season of giving, I’d like to speak about the gifts I’ve received from a most unlikely source: The COVID-19 virus. No, I haven’t contracted the virus, but I’ve been privileged to live through all the turmoil of 2020, from the tumultuous days of March’s Lockdown, the hopeful days of June’s Protests for racial equality, to the craziness of November and December’s Election Season. Like most people, I wish 2020 had turned out differently, but this unique year has given me some unique gifts. Let me share three of these with you.

**The Lack of Travel**

I was in Moab, Utah when the first emails came through in March about the changes COVID would bring. I was using that trip a as professional opportunity to get away and focus on my writing work. At that time, 2020 was set to include trips to Berlin, London, Sun Valley, Tanzania, and Whistler. Those trips all went “poof” over the course of a few days. I’m extremely grateful that I had only purchased tickets for one trip—the one to Whistler.

Professionally, COVID gave me the gift of some great lessons. First, always use a travel agent. My travel agent told me not to cancel my flight to Whistler and book the credit with the airline but wait for the airline to cancel the flight and give me a full refund. Cha-Ching. Second, I’ll be far more judicious about professional travel in the future. Spending hundreds (or thousands) of dollars and days to travel someplace for a one-hour meeting probably isn’t worth it. I’ve learned to be far more selective about when it really matters to travel.

The lessons continue in the personal sphere. I missed out on the opportunity to see some great places in the world this year, and I owe my wife all those cancelled trips. I don’t think I’ll look at them as a chore but a great opportunity to see some great new (and old) things. I’ve also reflected on how the places I’ve been – from cities like Chicago to Cape Town and over five continents – have helped me understand the world’s varying responses to COVID-19. Each place faced COVID with unique circumstances and cultural traditions, and each had to navigate their own path in dealing with the Virus.

**The Lack of a Commute**

COVID has shaved hours off my commute, and I’ve really enjoyed this great gift. For most of the past nine months, I’ve gone from commuting three times a week to three times a month. I’ve loved the savings in gas, and the amount of time I’ve saved (it’s an hour each way for me) has tremendously improved my professional productivity. It’s been good for my blood pressure to not participate in all the silliness of people fighting each other over one or two car-lengths in a traffic jam. My nose and lungs have also benefited from cleaner air.

In March and April, there were so few people on the roads that the few of us on the road at any given time had a clear path. I admit that I’ll miss the “Indy 500” that was I-15 during the early days of the commute. You could drive as fast as you wanted without having to pass anyone! What I won’t miss, however, are the people who haven’t taken their foot off the gas pedal since March: they think a full freeway should still move aside as they cruise past at 90+ MPH.

**The Privilege of Human Interaction**

I’m a teacher in my day job, and one thing I realized pretty quickly was that my students had been knocked off kilter by the pandemic. I’ve been around this mulberry bush before, from the great Recession of the early 1980s, 9/11, the 2008 financial meltdown, and other challenges. For many of my students, this is their first rodeo. What they needed was someone to listen to them as they talked through much of that impact. Some had lost jobs, some had seen wedding plans get altered, and most saw a bright and clear future fade into a haziness dizziness.

COVID’s gift to me was the reality that my students, and others, had little need for my technical knowledge, but they could benefit very much from a listening ear and some accumulated life lessons and wisdom. I did that for students in the spring and the fall, and I estimated the other day that I spent just shy of a full work-week of time listening and talking with others about what concerned them most during the pandemic. A bonus gift from COVID was the perspective to see that doing this was not a waste of time. In fact, it might have been the most important work I did this year.

I also had the privilege of teaching my MBA and Executive students live and in-person this fall. Even though we wore masks and sat in rooms with crazy seating configurations, it was amazingly nice to be with other people. The power of gathering together became clear when we had to meet in online sessions—they just weren’t the same. It will be a long time before I moan about having to be with people in a live setting. It’s a great part of being human.

\
I hope this Christmas treats you well and that Santa is good to you. I’d also remind you to remember the “reason for the season”—that we as Christians celebrate God’s great gift of His son to redeem us from our sins and guide us home to Him. May His rich blessings rain down upon you and your loved ones this season and as you prepare for 2021.

Merry Christmas!
