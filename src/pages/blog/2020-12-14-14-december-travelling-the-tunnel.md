Last week I wrote about the light at the end of the COVID tunnel. We now have efficacious vaccines. Much has happened over the past week to show us how muddled and uncertain this process will surely be. First, the FDA has approved the vaccine in the US, the UK has approved the vaccine and begun immunizations, and Canada has approved the vaccine. The US learned that supplies of the vaccine are being bought by other countries. Finally, we’ve seen the first vaccine warning in the UK; people with severe allergies are cautioned against taking the vaccine.

While the nature of many of these events was predictable (other nations lining up to buy vaccines), the fact that navigating the last of the tunnel will be a bumpy ride should surprise no one. We should be used to, and continue to expect, uncertainty and ambiguity as we trudge through the COVID tunnel toward spring and summer. Here are two things wise executives will keep in mind as they move forward.

**Cash is king, but . . .**

Most business leaders have, wisely, cut back on discretionary expenditures and held off on capital investments to preserve cash. Before we had the reality of the vaccine, estimates for reaching herd immunity—and a return to a mask-less, non-distanced world, ranged from 18 months to 4 years. With such a restrictive timeline, following the rule that cash is king, and wanting to have as much of it as possible made perfect sense. It’s not time to go on a spending spree, but it’s time to think hard about when, and for what, to open the spigots and begin spending.

The first issue here is timing. Capital expenditures, or even expenses, with maturity dates greater than 6-9 months deserve serious consideration. Purchasing new machinery, developing new processes, or investing to bring products to market next summer or fall are all wise uses of capital right now. When restrictions ease and customers feel like spending again (B2B or B2C), you want to be ready to satisfy that demand. There’s a good chance of a solid spending splurge, so be ready.

The second question surrounds what to spend money on right now. There’s a one-word answer: people. If your people have hunkered down to get through the crisis, now is the time to focus on building their skills through training and development. We’ll see the contours of what the post-COVID workplace looks like, but upskilled employees will help you recover and grow more quickly. You should also consider a hiring spree. At the entry level, there are two years of college graduates hungry for work. At the higher end, now might be a great time to convince someone to join your organization.

**Bottom line:** As the world turns to winter, smart executives will turn their attention to making smart investments which will enhance their competitive advantage next summer and fall.

**Measure twice, cut once**

Over the next several weeks we should see some clarity about how the vaccination timeline is going to roll out. I’d suggest adding an extra month to any predictions before April, and a couple of weeks for any timelines during Q2. There will be a tremendous rush to “reopen.” This will include bringing employees back to the office, resuming operations, and ramping up production schedules. Put simply, there’s pressure to get back to normal as quickly as possible.

My days in construction taught me the value of “measure twice, cut once.” It’s so easy to get ahead of ourselves and measure with imprecision, or transpose numbers, or something like that. Once we cut the board too short, we realize there’s no board stretcher: We have to cut another board. And remember this: the probability that you cut the board short increases 1) if it’s your last board, and/or 2) if the lumber store is about to close. Measuring twice takes a bit more time up front but it saves time over the long term. Always.

What does this mean for your business? Consider the real costs of bringing your people back to the office or allowing them to travel as soon as possible. Don’t bring your employees back just to send them home two weeks later. It’s bad for morale and productivity. Don’t send teams on the road and put them in harms way. Just wait. I’m talking about an extra couple of weeks or a month here. You’ll worry about losing out to fast-moving competitors, but you’ll cement the trust you have with your employees, key customers, and other stakeholders. Let your wisdom sine through your patience and concern for everyone’s well-being.

**Bottom Line:** We’re all sick of the phrase “an abundance of caution.” Don’t be. Continue to employ that abundance as we move through the tunnel. It’s not about being first out of the tunnel, it’s about staying out of the tunnel once you leave.

The excitement of a new vaccine will continue to give way to the sobering reality of the difficulty of immunizing an entire nation in short order. Expect bumps in the road; expect the tunnel to be longer than you think it will, or should, be. Plan for things to truly move forward next fall and take steps now to be well-positioned then.
