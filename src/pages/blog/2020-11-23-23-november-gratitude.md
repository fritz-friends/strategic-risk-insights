Thanksgiving is far and away my favorite holiday. It’s the one that’s proved most resilient to commercialization, except for the grocers who profit selling turkey and all the other great things that make up a feast. I love checking out the Macy’s Thanksgiving Day Parade, watching the Lion’s game, spending the day with family, and most of all giving thanks. As we sit down to eat, I’ll often ask each person to express something they’re grateful for.

The scientific benefits of gratitude are well known. People who express gratitude through a daily gratitude journal enjoy greater overall health, they sleep better, have fewer mental health issues, have higher self-esteem, and enjoy deeper, more fulfilling relationships. The power of gratitude comes from its twofold nature. First, gratitude forces us to look for the good in the world. My gratitude journal often contains entries like “beautiful sunset last night,” “beautiful sunrise this morning,” “rain,” “snow,” or “sunshine,” depending on the season. Gratitude helps us look past the bad and focus on the good.

Second, gratitude is something that happens to us, we’re rarely grateful for things we do for ourselves. It might be that we get a long-awaited promotion, someone provides key help on a project at work, a stranger lets us merge on the freeway without a fight, or a kind word or compliment from someone. Gratitude gets us outside ourselves, it turns our attention to others and their contributions to our lives. Giving thanks is powerful medicine that can cure much of what ails us.

Given the challenges we’ve experienced in 2020, it may seem there’s not much to be grateful for, but here’s my list for 2020. I’m grateful for

**My relationship with my wife:** The spring-time lockdown would have been unbearable without her. I’m so glad that she’s my partner, supportive in all the best ways. She gives encouragement to things I ought to do and helps me see the folly of things I should not.

**Our five children:** They’ve shown remarkable resilience over the year and have refused to give in to the anxiety of the pandemic, and they’ve acquitted themselves well among social unrest, political division, and the vitriolic outbursts of frustration I’ve seen from many others. I’m grateful that they have level heads and have continued to move forward during a crazy year.

**Best in class science and pharmaceutical research:** Science is not knowledge; it is a method for gaining knowledge. Those who thought that knowledge about the virus should come immediately were disappointed, but so are those who believed we’d never figure out what’s going on or what to do. The vaccine candidates look promising. Good vaccines are 50-70% effective, and we’re looking at two vaccines that are 95% effective.

**Health care workers and others giving compassionate care:** The pandemic has stretched the health care system in ways few of us could imagine. We’ve seen where our system stumbles and we now see areas for improvement. The pandemic has also laid bare the level of compassion that those who work in health care have for those they treat. Many have worked, and continue to work, extra shifts to the point of exhaustion. There are home caregivers, home health aides, and hospice workers who do the same. Kudo’s to all of you.

**A (relatively) resilient and robust economy:** When the March and April unemployment figures rolled in, we saw spikes in job losses that portended an awful economic future. It seemed for a while that we’d have to figure out a word worse than depression to categorize this economy. While the number of new unemployment claims remains shockingly high, and some industries continue to see economic devastation, the overall unemployment rate continues to decline. We aren’t out of the woods yet, but the US economy continues to move forward. People are returning to work, and for many, some semblance of normalcy will continue.

**The American Spirit:** Earlier this spring, I often used a quote from Warren Buffet, the fabled Oracle of Omaha: *“The American miracle, the American magic has always prevailed, and it will do so again.”* For me, the American Spirit is built on three foundations. The first is hard work. Most of our success just comes from diligent, sustained effort. The second is innovation. When we find a problem that can’t be solved with what we have, we tinker and experiment until we find something that works. The third is faith in the future. The promise of America is that tomorrow can be better than today. I’m grateful for that spirit which sustains me.

The 100th Psalm is known as the Thanksgiving Psalm. It seems fitting to leave you with these words that have inspired people for millennia:

**PSALM 100**

A Psalm of praise.

**1** Make a joyful noise unto the Lord, all ye lands.

**2** Serve the Lord with gladness: come before his presence with singing.

**3** Know ye that the Lord he *is* God: *it is* he *that* hath made us, and not we ourselves; *we are* his people, and the sheep of his pasture.

**4** Enter into his gates with thanksgiving, *and* into his courts with praise: be thankful unto him, *and* bless his name.

**5** For the Lord *is* good; his mercy *is* everlasting; and his truth *endureth* to all generations.
