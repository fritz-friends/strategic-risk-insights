Given the current moment, I want to reflect on the ongoing United States election. I want to isolate some very foundational elements of the American political system that have been on display this week. As I watched our system operate this past week, I saw a system that accounted for significant political differences but that did not devolve into dysfunctional violence.

I had read with interest many pre-election scenarios and speculations regarding the potential for violence and disruption. Some predicted chaos that would topple the constitutional system that is the American Experience. Predictions ran the gamut from massive protests and personal fistfights to the sudden mobilization of private militias and armed insurrection. All these things *may* *still* happen; however, given what’s *already happened* since election day, many of those apocalyptic scenarios seem increasingly unlikely.

I’ve seen the strength of our institutions in three areas: fundamental respect for individual rights, a respect for the rules of the game, and a respect for reasoned and measured discourse.

**Respect for Individual Rights**

I went to my office on Tuesday unsure of what I’d find on my way home in terms of voting-related violence. As I checked my news feed throughout the day, I was increasingly encouraged at pictures of long voting lines—socially distanced of course—and what appeared to be an overwhelmingly peaceful process. As I listened to the news reports that night, I heard about voters sporting bright red MAGA hats standing next to folks in deep blue “I’m Ridin’ with Biden” shirts. While their views about the best way forward differed, the election reaffirmed the sanctity of the right every registered voter has to express his or her views at the ballot box.

While we may be polarized around solutions, we seem to retain a fundamental unity around the sanctity of the vote and the electoral process. There are deep disagreements regarding the perspective of what our country should be and the policies that will get us there, but there’s a fundamental agreement that having differences of opinion, and the right to hold and express those opinions, is fundamental to the American experience.

**Bottom Line:** We need to pay attention to differing opinions and accept conflicting beliefs and values; we shouldn’t pretend they don’t exist. We can’t forget that which unites us; whether that be a common vision of basic rights, or a shared commitment to a process that values differing opinions.

**Respect for the Rules of the Game**

The American electoral system has been called every name in the book, from archaic, confusing, and clunky, to a patchwork of precedents that produce punctuated results. Electoral chaos stems from one of the fundamental rules of the US system, a rule that provides great strength, but not always efficiency. Elections, even those for national office, are state affairs, and each state has the freedom to set its own policies, procedures, and rules for elections. In this respect, the Founders continue to rest easy—the productive tension between the efficiency of a unified central government and the local legitimacy of a decentralized system lives on. No one has questioned the fundamental role of states and localities in administering elections.

Respect for the rules has another key component: respect for the rule of law. In my community there was an incident on Tuesday night that exemplified this. A group of protestors appeared across the street from the County Offices. Within a short period of time, a group of counter protestors, armed with their own weapons, appeared. There was respect for the right to assemble and respect for the right to bear arms. Both groups exercised their rights, but neither went beyond their rights into unlawful violence. Even those contesting election results have done so through courts of law.

**Bottom line:** When respect for law abounds, political and ideological pluralism becomes a source of strength rather than a one of weakness. The same is true in business—when the rules of the game are clear and respected, everyone benefits.

**Respect for Reason**

All the doomsday scenarios required the rule of reason to give way to mob rule. 2020 has stretched our reliance on reason to the limit. We’ve seen scientists and health professionals disagree over the nature of COVID-19 and how best to respond, but those debates have been marked by reason and evidence. We’ve also seen protests for social justice and an end to systemic racism that have featured reasoned arguments and measured calls for meaningful change.

We’ve also seen the opposite behaviors of mob rule: fear-driven responses to the pandemic and anger-driven destruction that went far beyond the reasonable limits of peaceful protest. 2020 has exposed some of our worst tendencies toward inflamed passion.

What we haven’t seen since the election is wide-spread abandonment of reason, but we have seen a commitment to the process of American Democracy. While some have expressed passion-driven outrage at results or the timing of results, those voices have been “lone wolves in the wilderness.” By and large, people across the political color spectrum have urged and exercised patience and restraint. The most reasoned response has been to not take the bait offered by those hoping to use passions broom to sweep away reason.

**Bottom Line:** The hallmark of humanity is our ability to reason. That’s true in politics, in our personal relationships, and our business lives. This week has shown me that reason still carries the day for most people, most of the time.

I don’t know what will happen next, but two things seem clear. First, Americans have, and will continue to have, different views of what type of society we’re trying to build. Those views are strongly held and represent a real challenge to unity and progress. Second, as an antidote, most Americans seem to be driven by a fundamental respect. Respect for each other, respect for rules and laws, and respect for the power of reason. That gives me hope that we’ll be able to continue to build something great.
