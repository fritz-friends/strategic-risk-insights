Last week’s post summarized my observations about the power of respect in our political system and election process. I noted that, in spite of much digital angst and ink spilt on doom and gloom scenarios, the US election seemed filled with respect. Respect for individuals, respect for the rules of the game, and respect for the calming power of reason.

In this week’s post, I’d like to consider how those three elements of fundamental respect play out in the business world, and how leaders can leverage the principle of respect to deepen their competitive advantage. In this day of uncertainty about so many things, respect stands as a bulwark, unchanging principle that can help you navigate the turbulent waters of today and the murky and unclear future that awaits us in 2021 and beyond. The bases of respect are the same: respect for individuals, respect for the processes that companies establish (the rules), and respect for the calming effect of reason, in this case being data driven.

**Respect for Individuals**

Most executives I know talk a good game in terms of their commitment to their people: “our people are our most important asset.” Unfortunately, too many of them fail to walk the walk. Whether its manufacturing specialists on the shop floor or functional specialists at the corporate head office, too many people are treated as depreciable assets. The first way to show respect for the individuals in your organization is to increase the level of training they get. Many companies have cut their training budget to conserve cash during 2020. Reverse course in 2021. The failure to invest in your people and their development is penny wise and pound foolish. You’ll need all the skills you can muster to win going forward.

The second way to show respect is to seek out and encourage the flourishing diversity in your workforce. This certainly means enhancing the ethnic, gender, and racial diversity of your workforce. It also means much more. True respect for diversity means bringing the diverse voices in your firm into the strategic decision-making process. Think about including production and maintenance workers on teams dealing with new product development, customer care, or other internal processes. The different perspective they’ll add will help you see issues more clearly, and your resulting plans will be more complete and more robust.

**Bottom line:** If people are your greatest asset, respect for their knowledge and perspective enhances their value to your business.

**Respect for Process**

Nobel prize winner Herbert Simon and his colleague James March made an interesting observation over 60 years ago: an organization’s structure and process represent its accumulated wisdom about how to solve problems. Structure encodes past learning about what works. Executives who respect those processes will think long and hard about making wholesale changes to internal processes in the face of a crisis like the coronavirus pandemic. Take globalizing your supply chain, for example. Your company spent years learning what works in a geographically decentralized supply chain. The pandemic has exposed several hidden risks to strategy, and many organizations are beginning to consider “onshoring.”

I’ve argued that many companies will think hard about “onshoring” much of their supply chain to reduce the long term strategic risks of another pandemic (or related crisis), but onshoring also provides protection against risks from political nationalism and the economic challenges of managing complexity. As you consider onshoring, or any other change to the strategic architecture of your business, ask yourself how you can keep the accumulated learning of solving past problems as you solve this one. Change the architecture in ways that preserves the learning encoded in current processes.

**Bottom line:** Respect what your organization has learned over time about problem solving. As you adjust the structural and process architecture of the firm, don’t throw the baby out with the bathwater.

**Respect for reason**

In last week’s post, I noted that most political leaders had pulled back from impassioned arguments that were driven by opinion and emotion and had spoken in measured terms that highlighted very reasonable issues that needed to be addressed. This has been true of both the blue and red teams. The business correlate here is to circumscribe emotional reactions to problem solving. We’ve lived with fear and the anxiety produced by uncertainty for almost 8 months now. Pandemic fatigue is setting in, and there’s a real risk to make decisions based on the emotions that accompany fatigue.

I teach my students a powerful way to overcome the negative impacts of emotion on decision-making, and to leverage the positive influence emotion offers to decision makers. The method is quite simple, and I learned it from Entrepreneurial guru Michael Gerber. There are three easy steps: 1) *Admit* the emotion you are feeling. Put a name to it and acknowledge what’s going on. 2) *Feel* it—give space in your mind and heart for the power of what you are feeling. Process your emotions. 3) *Don’t be driven* by it. You can be frustrated and want action, but act based on reason, not frustration.

**Bottom Line:** We all have emotions, it’s a fact of human life. You’ll make better decisions when you account for and accommodate emotion than when you try to deny it and pretend it doesn’t exist.

We’re rounding the corner on 2020 and headed into 2021. Thanksgiving is coming in a week, and what that means for most businesses is that strategic planning for 2021 will reach its final stages, where key decisions and budgets will be finalized. As your firm “sets in stone” its plans for the next year or eighteen months, make sure you have doused your planning with liberal doses of respect. For your people, your processes, and the role of deliberative reason. When applied liberally, the principle of respect will improve organizational performance and individual satisfaction.
